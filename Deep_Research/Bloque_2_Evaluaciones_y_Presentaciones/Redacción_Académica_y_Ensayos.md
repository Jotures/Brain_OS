# Redacción Académica y Ensayos: The Comprehensive Handbook on Economic Rhetoric, Structure, and Citation Strategy
## Part I: The Epistemological Foundations of Economic Writing

### 1.1 The Rhetoric of Economics: Writing as a Method of Inquiry

Economics occupies a distinct and often misunderstood position within the academy, oscillating between the rigorous formalism of the physical sciences and the interpretive nuance of the humanities. This duality fundamentally shapes the discipline's approach to writing. It is not merely a vehicle for reporting data; it is the primary instrument of persuasion and, more critically, of discovery. As articulated in foundational guidance from Harvard University, writing in economics is never simply a matter of asserting opinions; it is the process of establishing professional credentials by demonstrating a mastery of facts, theories, and models. The "rhetoric of economics," a concept championed by scholars like Deirdre McCloskey, posits that economists utilize specific literary devices—mathematical models, statistical significance tests, and historical narratives—to persuade their audience of a particular truth. Thus, the text itself is not separate from the economics; the writing *is* the economics.

A prevalent misconception among novice researchers is that writing is a post-hoc activity, a task to be undertaken only after the "real work" of running regressions or solving models is complete. Expert consensus, however, refutes this dichotomy. Duke University’s pedagogical resources emphasize that "writing is thinking". The act of forcing abstract mathematical concepts or complex empirical findings into linear, coherent prose often reveals latent gaps in logic, missing assumptions, or inconsistencies in the argument. When a researcher struggles to draft a clear Introduction, it is rarely a symptom of poor writing skills alone; it is frequently a diagnostic signal that the research question itself is ill-defined. Similarly, an inability to write a cohesive Discussion section often indicates a failure to fully grasp the implications of the empirical results. Therefore, mastery of academic writing is indistinguishable from mastery of economic analysis itself.

The primary objective of economic writing is "economical" prose—writing that respects the reader's time by conveying complex ideas with minimal linguistic friction. This stylistic imperative distinguishes economics from literary criticism, which may value ambiguity, or legal writing, which relies on repetitive precision. An economist must tell a story about the data, weaving technical findings into a narrative that explains human behavior. The goal is clarity and efficiency, ensuring that the sophisticated machinery of the model does not obscure the economic intuition.

### 1.2 The Audience: Navigating the Specialist-Generalist Divide

Writing effectively requires a precise and empathetic understanding of the audience. In economics, the audience is frequently bifurcated, creating a unique rhetorical challenge. On one hand, the technical details—the derivation of the model, the proof of the theorem, the specifics of the identification strategy—must satisfy the intense scrutiny of specialized referees. These peers demand rigor, mathematical precision, and reproducibility. On the other hand, the paper’s "punchline," its motivation, and its policy implications must be accessible to a broader audience that includes generalist economists, policymakers, and occasionally the lay public.

Successful papers navigate this tension by employing a strategic "hourglass" structure. The Introduction and Conclusion are written for the generalist, focusing on intuition, contribution, and relevance. The internal sections—Theoretical Framework and Empirical Strategy—are written for the specialist, providing the necessary technical detail to ensure robustness. This structure ensures that a reader can grasp the paper's significance without necessarily wading through the densest technical proofs, while the specialist can find the rigorous evidence required to validate the claims.

Furthermore, the tone of economic writing has evolved. The "scientific" passive voice of the mid-20th century, which sought to create an impression of objective detachment ("It was found that..."), is increasingly rejected in favor of a direct, active style ("We find that..."). This shift reflects a recognition that research is an active process of inquiry conducted by human agents. However, the tone must remain professional and objective; it is a delicate balance of engagement and detachment, avoiding both the dryness of a technical manual and the informality of a blog post.

## Part II: The Architecture of Empirical Research

The empirical paper has become the dominant form in contemporary economics, driven by the "credibility revolution" and the explosion of administrative data. Its structure has become highly standardized, a rigidity that serves to facilitate rapid information retrieval by readers who know exactly where to look for specific details.

### 2.1 The Abstract: The Executive Summary of the Academy

The abstract is the "foot-in-the-door" for any research paper. In a world of information overload, it is often the only part of the paper that is read. Consequently, it must be crafted with extreme care. Unlike abstracts in some other disciplines which may be vague teasers, an economics abstract must be a self-contained summary of the entire project.

A successful abstract in empirical economics, particularly in fields like development or labor, typically follows a strict four-part formula:

1. **Motivation/Question:** The opening sentence should state the broad problem or specific research question. It sets the context immediately.
2. **Methodology:** The abstract must briefly describe the data and the identification strategy. It is insufficient to say "we analyze the data"; one must specify "Using a difference-in-differences approach with administrative tax records..." This signals the paper's methodological rigor.
3. **Key Results:** The core findings must be stated explicitly, including the direction and, crucially, the *magnitude* of the effects. Vague statements like "results are mixed" are to be avoided.
4. **Implications:** The final sentence should connect the specific findings back to the broader economic theory or policy landscape.

Crucially, the abstract should typically not contain citations unless the paper’s primary contribution is refuting a specific, famous result. It must be intelligible on its own, without reference to the main text or bibliography.

### 2.2 The Introduction: The Inverted Pyramid

The Introduction is arguably the most critical section of the paper. It is the sales pitch. If the main argument is buried or the contribution is unclear, readers—and editors—may never proceed to the evidence. The structure should resemble an inverted pyramid, moving from the general to the specific.

**The Hook and the Context:** The introduction should begin with the "hook"—the broad economic issue or puzzle that motivates the study. Why should the reader care? This is not the place for a literature review; it is the place to establish the stakes.
**The Specific Question:** The narrative must then narrow quickly to the precise research question.
**The Contribution:** This is the pivot point. The author must explicitly state how this paper advances knowledge. Does it use a new, richer dataset? Does it apply a novel methodological technique? Does it resolve a theoretical ambiguity?.**The Punchline:** In economics, unlike in mystery novels, the ending must be revealed immediately. The "punchline"—the main finding—should be featured prominently in the first few paragraphs. This allows the reader to evaluate the evidence in light of the claimed result.
**The Roadmap:** The final paragraph of the introduction conventionally provides a roadmap of the remaining sections ("Section 2 describes the data, Section 3 presents the model..."). While formulaic, this signposting helps the reader navigate the subsequent technical material.

### 2.3 The Data Section: Establishing Credibility

In empirical papers, the Data section is where the researcher establishes the validity of their laboratory. It must go beyond a simple list of sources to a detailed description of the data generation process.

**Source Identification:** The author must clearly identify the source of the data, explaining why this specific source is appropriate for the research question. Whether it is administrative records, survey data, or scraped web data, the provenance must be transparent.**Sample Selection:** A critical component is the description of how the final analysis sample was constructed. Which observations were dropped and why? Was it due to missing values, outliers, or specific exclusion criteria? This transparency is vital for assessing potential selection bias.
**Descriptive Statistics:** A "Table 1" is standard in almost all empirical papers. This table presents the means, standard deviations, and sample sizes of the key variables. It provides the reader with a snapshot of the "world" the paper is analyzing.**Caveats and Limitations:** Honest scholarship requires a frank discussion of the data's limitations. Issues such as measurement error, top-coding, or limited geographic coverage should be acknowledged upfront. This preempts criticism and demonstrates intellectual integrity.

### 2.4 Methodology and Identification Strategy

This section is the intellectual core of the empirical paper. It links the economic theory to the statistical test. The goal is to convince the reader that the estimated coefficients represent causal relationships, not merely correlations.

The writer must explain the "identification strategy"—the logical argument for why the observed relationship is causal. This often involves justifying key assumptions, such as the "parallel trends" assumption in difference-in-differences designs or the exogeneity of instruments in instrumental variables (IV) regression. The transition from the economic model (the theoretical relationship) to the econometric model (the regression equation) must be explicit. The author should write out the equation to be estimated, defining every subscript and variable clearly.

### 2.5 Results: The Narrative of Evidence

The Results section should be organized around the testing of hypotheses, not merely as a "data dump." The text should guide the reader through the tables, highlighting the most important coefficients and patterns.

**Main Estimates:** The core findings should be presented in clear, well-formatted tables. The text should interpret these estimates, discussing their economic magnitude, not just their statistical significance. A result can be statistically significant but economically trivial; the writer must distinguish between the two.**Robustness Checks:** To demonstrate the stability of the findings, the author should present "robustness checks." These are alternative specifications (e.g., adding different controls, using different subsamples) that show the result is not a fluke of a specific modeling choice. This is a hallmark of rigorous economic scholarship.

### 2.6 Conclusion: Synthesis and Implications

The Conclusion should not merely summarize the paper; the Abstract has already done that. Instead, it should synthesize the findings into a coherent argument and discuss their broader implications. This is the place to discuss policy relevance, external validity (do these results apply elsewhere?), and directions for future research. It is the final opportunity to frame the paper's contribution to the discipline.

## Part III: The Architecture of Theoretical Research

Theoretical papers differ significantly in structure from their empirical counterparts. They prioritize the logical consistency of the model and the elegance of the proof over data description. The writing must be precise, as the ambiguity of natural language can be fatal to a mathematical argument.

### 3.1 The Introduction in Theory

Like empirical papers, theoretical papers begin with an introduction that motivates the puzzle. However, the "contribution" here is often a new modeling technique, a generalization of an existing result, or a counter-intuitive finding derived from first principles. The introduction must set the stage for the model, explaining *why* a theoretical approach is necessary to understand the phenomenon.

### 3.2 The Model Setup: Primitives and Assumptions

The body of a theoretical paper begins with the "Model" or "Setup." This section defines the "primitives" of the economy: the agents (consumers, firms), their preferences (utility functions), the technology (production functions), and the endowments.
**Ordering Assumptions:** William Thomson’s guide advises stating assumptions in order of decreasing plausibility or generality. Crucially, the writer must distinguish between "simplifying assumptions" (made for tractability) and "critical assumptions" (without which the result falls apart). This transparency allows the reader to assess the fragility of the model.**Notation:** Consistent and mnemonic notation is vital. Use $c$ for cost, $p$ for price, $w$ for wage. Introduce symbols only when they are needed, and define them immediately. A paper cluttered with undefined or inconsistent notation is unreadable.

### 3.3 Theorems and Proofs

The main findings of a theoretical paper are presented as "Propositions," "Lemmas," or "Theorems."
**Propositions:** These are the key economic insights derived from the model.
**Proofs:** The mathematical derivation that validates the proposition. Standard practice in economics is to place long or complex proofs in an Appendix, keeping the main text focused on the economic intuition. However, a sketch of the proof or the intuition behind it should remain in the main text to guide the reader.**Intuition:** For every theorem, there must be a paragraph explaining the "economic intuition." Why does the result hold? Is it due to an income effect dominating a substitution effect? This verbal explanation is as important as the mathematical proof; it bridges the gap between math and economics.

### 3.4 Extensions and Robustness

Theoretical papers often include an "Extensions" section where the author relaxes specific assumptions to see if the main results hold. This corresponds to the "robustness checks" of empirical papers. It demonstrates the generality of the theory and preempts criticism about the model being too "special" or specific.

## Part IV: The Art of the Literature Review

A Literature Review in economics can function either as a standalone survey article (such as those published in the *Journal of Economic Literature*) or as a distinct section within a research paper. In both contexts, its primary function is synthesis, not mere listing.

### 4.1 Synthesis and Thematic Organization

A common error is to organize the literature review chronologically, producing a "he said, she said" list of summaries. Expert guidance from the *Journal of Economic Literature* and university writing centers advises organizing by *theme*, *methodology*, or *debate*. The writer should group papers that share a common approach or finding and analyze them together.**Critical Analysis:** The review must be critical. It is insufficient to state that "Smith (2010) found X." The writer must explain *why* Smith found X—perhaps due to a specific dataset, a unique time period, or a particular identifying assumption—and how that contrasts with Jones (2012) who found Y. The review should illuminate the strengths and weaknesses of the existing body of work.

### 4.2 Identifying the Gap

The ultimate goal of the literature review in a research paper is to identify the "gap"—the missing piece of the puzzle that the current paper intends to fill. The review should culminate in a statement of what is known and what remains unknown, setting the stage for the paper's contribution.

### 4.3 JEL Survey Guidelines

For standalone survey articles, the standards are even higher. A successful JEL article must clearly address "why we should care," be accessible to generalist economists, and provide a selective, synthetic coverage of the relevant literature. It is not an exhaustive catalog of every paper ever written; it is a curated guide that imposes structure on a chaotic field.

## Part V: Stylistic Mechanics and Economical Prose

The style of economic writing is a tool for clarity. The discipline has moved away from the opaque, bureaucratic style of the past toward a "plain English" approach that values precision and vigor.

### 5.1 Active vs. Passive Voice

Historically, academic writing relied heavily on the passive voice ("The data were analyzed...") to convey a sense of objective detachment. However, modern style guides, including those from the *Journal of Economic Literature* and Duke University, strongly advocate for the active voice ("We analyzed the data...").**Why Active Voice?**

- **Clarity:** It clearly identifies the actor. "The central bank raised rates" is clearer than "Rates were raised."
- **Conciseness:** Active sentences are almost always shorter and more direct.
- **Vigor:** Active verbs propel the reader forward, making the text more engaging.**When to Use Passive Voice:** The passive voice remains useful when the agent is unknown or irrelevant ("The data were collected in 2010") or to maintain "topic continuity" (keeping the focus on the object of the previous sentence). However, it should be the exception, not the rule.

### 5.2 The Scourge of Nominalizations

Nominalizations are verbs or adjectives that have been turned into nouns (e.g., "maximization" from "maximize," "introduction" from "introduce"). They are often referred to as "zombie nouns" because they suck the life out of a sentence.

- **Weak:** "The *implementation* of the policy caused a *reduction* in unemployment."
- **Strong:** "Implementing the policy *reduced* unemployment.".
Overusing nominalizations leads to heavy, bureaucratic prose that is difficult to parse. Converting these nouns back into verbs makes the writing more dynamic and easier to understand.

### 5.3 Clarity and Precision

Economics deals with precise concepts (elasticity, marginal utility, equilibrium). Writers must use these terms correctly but avoid unnecessary jargon that obscures meaning.

- **The Ambiguous "This":** Economists often use the word "this" as a standalone subject ("This proves that..."). This is a dangerous habit that creates ambiguity. Writers should always attach a noun: "This *result* proves that..." or "This *assumption* implies...".
- **Signposting:** Because economic arguments can be complex, explicit "signposting" is necessary. Phrases like "In the next section, we..." or "Conversely..." guide the reader through the logical structure. However, avoid "throat-clearing" phrases like "It is interesting to note that..."; just state the interesting fact directly.

### 5.4 Logical Pitfalls

Writers must be vigilant against common logical fallacies that undermine economic arguments.

- **Correlation vs. Causation:** The cardinal sin in economics. Be extremely careful with causal language ("causes," "impacts," "drivers") unless the identification strategy supports it. Use "is associated with" or "correlates with" for observational data.
- **Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc:** Assuming that because event B followed event A, A caused B.
- **The Fallacy of Composition:** Assuming what is true for the part is true for the whole (a common error in macroeconomics).

## Part VI: Visual Rhetoric – Tables and Figures

In empirical economics, tables and figures are not merely illustrations; they are the primary evidence. A reader should be able to understand the main findings of a paper by looking *only* at the tables and figures, without reading the text. This requirement dictates strict design principles.

### 6.1 Designing Effective Tables

Tables must be self-contained. The title should be descriptive, and the notes should define all variables and abbreviations.

**Key Elements of a Professional Table:**

- **The Title:** Concise and active. "Table 1: The Effect of Education on Wages" is standard.
- **The Stub:** The leftmost column listing the variables or categories. It should be left-aligned.
- **Column Heads:** These should be clearly labeled and centered over the data columns.
- **Significant Figures:** Do not report computer output raw (e.g., 0.0012345). Round to meaningful digits (usually two or three decimal places) to avoid false precision.
- **Standard Errors:** Usually reported in parentheses below the coefficient. The significance level is often indicated by asterisks (* p<0.1, ** p<0.05, *** p<0.01), though this practice is debated in some circles preferring exact p-values.
- **Notes:** The bottom of the table must include the source of data, definitions of the dependent variable, and details of the estimation method (e.g., "OLS regressions include state fixed effects").

**Formatting Rules:**

- **No Vertical Lines:** Vertical lines clutter the visual flow and are generally forbidden in professional typesetting. Use horizontal rules to separate the header from the body and the body from the footer.
- **Spanners:** Group related columns with "spanners" (headings that span multiple columns) to organize data hierarchically.

### 6.2 Figures and Data Visualization

Figures are preferred for showing trends, distributions, or non-linear relationships.

- **Simplicity:** Avoid "chart junk" like 3D effects, shadows, or distracting backgrounds. The "ink-to-data ratio" should be minimized (Tufte’s principle).
- **Captions vs. Titles:** In published papers, the "title" often appears as a caption below the figure. This caption should include a brief description of what the figure shows (e.g., "Figure 1 plots the relationship between GDP per capita and life expectancy in 2020").
- **Accessibility:** Alt-text should be provided for screen readers, describing the content and trend of the graph objectively (e.g., "Line graph showing a steady increase in inflation from 2% in 2015 to 5% in 2022").

## Part VII: Citation Management and Integrity

Proper citation is the bedrock of academic integrity. In economics, this involves not just citing ideas and text, but also datasets and working papers. The complexity of modern research often necessitates the use of citation management software.

### 7.1 Citation Styles in Economics

Unlike psychology (APA) or literature (MLA), economics does not have a single, universal citation style. However, the **Chicago Manual of Style (Author-Date system)** is the most common basis, often modified by specific journals (e.g., the *American Economic Review* style).

**Common Conventions:**

- **In-text:** Parenthetical citations are standard: (Smith 2010) or "Smith (2010) argues...".
- **Bibliography:** Alphabetical order by author.
- **Working Papers:** Citations for NBER or IZA working papers are ubiquitous in economics. They must include the series number: "Smith, J. (2020). 'The Economics of Writing.' NBER Working Paper No. 12345.".

### 7.2 Citing Data and Code

The "replication crisis" has elevated the importance of citing data. It is no longer sufficient to say "we used World Bank data." A formal citation is required.

**Format:** Author/Organization. Year. *Title of Dataset*. Version. Persistent Identifier (DOI or URL).

- *Example:* U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics. 2023. "Employment Situation Summary.". Accessed March 15, 2023. [https://www.bls.gov](https://www.bls.gov/)....**FRED Data:** The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis provides specific citation guidelines for data downloaded from FRED, ensuring that the original source and the aggregator are credited.

### 7.3 Reference Management Tools

Managing hundreds of citations requires software. Two tools dominate the market for economists: **Zotero** and **Mendeley**.

- **Zotero:** Open-source, excellent at capturing metadata from web browsers, and integrates well with Google Docs and Word. It has a strong plugin ecosystem (e.g., Better BibTeX) which is crucial for LaTeX users.
- **Mendeley:** Owned by Elsevier, it has strong PDF management and annotation features. It is often preferred by those who want a social networking aspect or cloud sync across devices.

## Part VIII: Technical Workflow – LaTeX and BibTeX

For theoretical papers or those with heavy econometrics, **LaTeX** is the standard word processor due to its superior handling of mathematics.

### 8.1 The LaTeX Ecosystem

LaTeX allows the writer to focus on content while the software handles formatting.

- **BibTeX:** This is the bibliography management tool for LaTeX. References are stored in a plain text `.bib` file.
- **Workflow:** Users export citations from Zotero/Mendeley into a `.bib` file. In the LaTeX document, a command like `\bibliography{references.bib}` generates the reference list automatically, formatted according to the style file (e.g., `\bibliographystyle{aea}` for American Economic Association journals).
- **Overleaf:** A cloud-based LaTeX editor that integrates directly with Zotero and Mendeley, simplifying the workflow for collaborative papers.

### 8.2 Formatting Tables in LaTeX

Creating professional tables in LaTeX can be daunting. The `booktabs` package is essential for producing publication-quality tables.

- **Top Rule:** `\toprule` (thick line at the top).
- **Mid Rule:** `\midrule` (thinner line separating headers).
- **Bottom Rule:** `\bottomrule` (thick line at the bottom).
- **Alignment:** Use `dcolumn` or `siunitx` packages to align numbers by the decimal point, which is crucial for readability when coefficients vary in magnitude.

## Part IX: The Revision and Publication Process

The first draft is rarely the final product. The revision phase is where a paper becomes "economical."

### 9.1 Editing for Conciseness

Revision involves cutting. Writers should look for:

- **Redundancies:** "Past history," "future plans," "unexpected surprise."
- **Empty Openers:** "It is important to note that," "There is a model that..." (Change to "The model...").
- **Prepositional Decay:** Sentences strung together with endless "of," "in," "by." Break them up or rewrite using active verbs.

### 9.2 Responding to Peer Review

When a paper is "Revise and Resubmit" (R&R), the author must write a "Response to Referees." This document is as important as the paper itself.

- **Tone:** Must be polite, professional, and grateful, even if the referee was harsh.
- **Structure:** Address every single point raised by the referees. Copy the referee's comment, then provide the response.
- **Action:** Indicate exactly what changed in the manuscript ("We have added a robustness check in Table 4..."). If rejecting a suggestion, explain why with strong evidence.

## Part X: Conclusion and Final Checklist

Academic writing in economics is a disciplined craft that merges mathematical rigor with persuasive narrative. It requires the researcher to be a master of two worlds: the technical world of models and data, and the rhetorical world of argumentation and storytelling. By adhering to the structures of empirical and theoretical papers, mastering the nuances of active and economical style, and utilizing modern tools for citation and data management, economists can ensure their work contributes effectively to the "market for ideas." The ultimate goal is not just to be correct, but to be understood—to transform complex economic reality into a text that is clear, coherent, and compelling.

**Final Rhetorical Audit:**

1. **The Title Test:** Does the title accurately reflect the specific question and method?
2. **The Abstract Test:** Does it contain the results, not just the plan?
3. **The Intro Test:** Is the "punchline" visible on the first or second page?
4. **The Paragraph Test:** Does every paragraph start with a clear topic sentence?
5. **The Table Test:** Can the tables be understood without reading the text?
6. **The Citation Test:** Are all datasets cited? Are working papers properly identified?

By systematically applying these principles, the economic researcher ensures that their work meets the highest standards of the discipline, bridging the gap between raw analysis and impactful scholarship.

## Table 2: Common Writing Pitfalls and Corrections

| **Pitfall** | **Example of Poor Writing** | **Correction (Economical Style)** | **Reason** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Nominalization** | "The *establishment* of the firm occurred in 1990." | "The firm was *established* in 1990." | Turns a static noun back into an active verb. |
| **Passive Voice** | "It was found by the authors that inflation rose." | "We found that inflation rose." | Identifies the agent and shortens the sentence. |
| **Ambiguous "This"** | "Rates fell. *This* implies that investment will rise." | "Rates fell. *This decline* implies that investment will rise." | Clarifies the subject of the implication. |
| **Throat Clearing** | "It is interesting to note that the results are significant." | "The results are significant." | Removes unnecessary filler words. |
| **False Causality** | "Education *causes* higher wages." (in OLS) | "Education *is associated with* higher wages." | Respects the limits of observational data. |
