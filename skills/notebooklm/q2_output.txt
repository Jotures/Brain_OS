**Sophisticated AI models completely fail at basic spatial reasoning, and scaling them up does not help.** Researchers found that the latest, highly advanced AI modelsÔÇöwhich can write poetry and solve complex mathematicsÔÇöhad a **0% success rate on simple spatial reasoning tasks that a child could master** [1]. Counterintuitively, the research revealed that an agent's exploration strategy has a "remarkably limited impact" on its final reasoning accuracy [2]. Instead, performance depends heavily on structured memory representation, and **spatial reasoning hits a hard capability ceiling that cannot be overcome simply by making the model bigger** [2, 3]. Similarly, a model's "epistemic robustness"ÔÇöits ability to maintain factual accuracy under stressÔÇöoperates independently of parameter count or architecture [3].

**In sequential multi-agent systems, agents naturally tend to become "lazy" and stop collaborating.** While multi-agent setups are designed to distribute complex reasoning, a phenomenon known as the "lazy agent" frequently emerges, where one agent contributes trivially (e.g., by just summarizing or copying) and forces the other to do all the work [4, 5]. Theoretical analysis reveals a surprising root cause: **the mathematical normalization terms used in reinforcement learning (specifically multi-turn GRPO) inadvertently penalize longer collaborative exchanges** [6, 7]. To minimize the number of turns, the system bypasses collaborative reflection entirely, causing the multi-agent framework to collapse into an ineffective single agent [4, 6].

**Naive "Random Drop" memory deletion can outperform highly sophisticated memory management.** When researchers tested how agents should "forget" old memories to save computational costs and preserve privacy, they compared complex strategies like Priority Decay and LRU (Least Recently Used) against simply dropping memories at random [8, 9]. Surprisingly, the **"Random Drop" policy achieved the highest overall composite score and the highest "Narrative Coherence"** [10-12]. The counterintuitive insight is that random eviction acts as a regularizer: it prevents the agent from getting stuck in loops where it repeatedly retrieves stale or self-contradictory fragments, whereas "smart" importance-aware policies often reinforce those rigid, noisy pathways [9, 12, 13].

**Most multi-agent systems fail due to specification and context sharing, not technical bugs.** While conventional wisdom focuses on the intelligence of the underlying large language model (LLM), field data shows that **41-87% of multi-agent LLM systems fail in production, with nearly 79% of those failures originating from specification and coordination issues** [14, 15]. The hardest unsolved problem is "context sharing"ÔÇöagents frequently operate with entirely different understandings of the current state, leading to disjointed workflows [16]. Furthermore, literature identifies an **"over-engineering trap"**: adding hierarchical multi-agent structures too early slows down iteration and obscures simple solutions [17]. For straightforward scenarios, a single agent is almost always preferable [17]. 

**Massive context windows do not solve the memory problem, and "forgetting" is a feature, not a bug.** It is a common misconception that simply increasing an LLM's context window (e.g., to 200,000+ tokens) eliminates the need for external memory architectures [18]. However, feeding an agent an ever-expanding transcript without explicit mechanisms for decay and consolidation degrades latency, increases retrieval noise, and causes agents to lose focus [18-20]. Instead, the most novel contributions suggest that **agents require biologically inspired "active forgetting" and "sleep" states** [21-23]. Just as the human brain prunes old connections to learn new skills, discarding trivial information is a deliberate design choice that ensures an agent remains sharp and avoids hallucination amplification [21, 24]. True agentic memory is not a passive database of chat logs, but a dynamic cycle of consolidation and pruning [25, 26].
